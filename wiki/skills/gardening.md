## Plants — Want to Grow

### Green Onions
- **Difficulty:** Very easy
- **Sun:** Full sun to partial shade
- **Water:** Keep soil moist, don't waterlog
- **Tips:**
  - Regrow store-bought green onions — trim and plant roots in soil or stand in a glass of water
  - "Cut and come again" — harvest from the top, leave the roots, they regrow
  - Container-friendly; great for a windowsill or small patio pot

### Shampoo Ginger Lily *(Zingiber zerumbet)*
- **Difficulty:** Easy in warm climates, moderate in containers
- **Sun:** Partial shade to full sun
- **Water:** Loves moisture; well-draining soil essential
- **Tips:**
  - The "shampoo" comes from squeezing the ripe cone-shaped flower head — produces a fragrant, soapy liquid. Traditional for hair wash.
  - Spreads via rhizomes — can become invasive if not contained; pot culture controls it
  - Tropical plant — needs warmth (USDA zone 8+). Bring indoors in cold weather.
  - Flowers start green, turn red/orange when ripe

### Tomatoes
- **Difficulty:** Moderate
- **Sun:** Full sun — minimum 6–8 hours
- **Water:** Consistent and deep. Inconsistent watering causes blossom end rot and skin cracking.
- **Tips:**
  - Plant deep — tomatoes can root from buried stem, creating a stronger plant
  - Stake or cage early; don't wait until they flop
  - Prune suckers (side shoots between stem and branch) on indeterminate varieties for bigger fruit
  - Common problems: blight (use resistant varieties), hornworms (pick off by hand), aphids (blast with water)
  - Don't water leaves — water at the base to prevent fungal disease

### Cabbage
- **Difficulty:** Easy to moderate
- **Sun:** Full sun
- **Water:** Consistent moisture; don't let it dry out
- **Tips:**
  - Cool-weather crop — best planted in spring or fall. Bolts (goes to seed) in summer heat.
  - Needs space: ~18 inches between plants for proper head formation
  - Common pests: cabbage loopers (green caterpillars), aphids, slugs
  - Row cover fabric keeps pests off without chemicals

## Wildlife Defense

### Birds
| Method | Notes |
|---|---|
| Bird netting | Most reliable — drape over crops, secure edges |
| Reflective Mylar tape | Hang strips to flutter in the wind; birds avoid the flashing light |
| Owl/hawk decoy | Works temporarily — move it regularly or birds learn to ignore it |
| Wind chimes | Light deterrent near bird-targeted plants |
| Row cover fabric | Blocks birds and most insects |

### Squirrels
| Method | Notes |
|---|---|
| Hardware cloth (wire mesh) | Line raised beds or cover with a cage; most reliable barrier |
| Cayenne pepper | Sprinkle on soil or plants — irritates their paws/nose, not harmful. Reapply after rain. |
| Motion-activated sprinkler | Best for open gardens; they hate getting wet |
| Raised bed with overhang | Squirrels can't grip a smooth overhang to climb in |

### General
- **Raised beds** — simplest all-in-one barrier for most small wildlife
- **Physical exclusion beats deterrents every time** — if something is eating your crop, cover it

## Best Practices

| Practice | Why |
|---|---|
| **Mulch everything** | Retains moisture, suppresses weeds, regulates soil temperature — 2–3 inches of compost or straw |
| **Water at the base** | Wet leaves promote fungal disease (blight, mildew) |
| **Crop rotation** | Don't grow same plant family in the same spot year after year — prevents nutrient depletion and soil pathogen buildup |
| **Companion planting** | Tomatoes + basil (pest deterrence), cabbage + dill (attracts beneficial wasps that eat pests) |
| **Compost amend soil** | Most veggies want pH 6.0–7.0, rich in organic matter |
| **Start seeds indoors** | Extend growing season — especially tomatoes and peppers |
| **Harvest regularly** | Encourages the plant to keep producing; leaving overripe fruit signals the plant to stop |
